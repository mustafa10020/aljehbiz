The Story of Skyscanner: From a Small Office to a Global Search Giant

Real story: three friends on a trip to the Scottish mountains wondered: why is it so hard to find cheap flights from the UK to Europe? Gareth Williams, Barry Smith, and Bonamy Grimes — software engineers — decided to solve the problem themselves. The year was 2001. The goal? Simple. Build a search engine that scans dozens of airline and booking sites simultaneously to show you the best price. No bias, no favoritism. Just prices. Thus, Skyscanner was born.

The challenge was enormous: obtaining flight data in 2001 meant negotiating with each airline separately — most refused. So the team built their own system to scrape data from airline websites legally. Over time, they developed direct partnerships with APIs — and today Skyscanner searches over 1,200 airlines and travel sites in seconds.

Skyscanner grew without any external funding for its first 6 years — relying solely on revenue from referral commissions. By 2010, it had 10 million monthly users. By 2015: 50 million. In 2017, the Chinese travel giant Trip.com Group (the Chinese parent of Ctrip) acquired Skyscanner for $1.75 billion — the largest exit in Scottish tech history. Despite the acquisition, Skyscanner continues to operate independently — and is still headquartered in Edinburgh. The Trip.com investment helped it expand faster into the Asian market — but its core remains the same as 2026.

Turning Point: In 2015, Skyscanner launched "Direct Booking" technology in partnership with Klarna — enabling users to book directly within the site without redirection. In 2023, it launched "Skyscanner Trips" — a smart trip organizer that syncs with your email to track all bookings automatically.

How Skyscanner Works — The Smartest Way to Search for Flights

Skyscanner's philosophy is different from Expedia or Booking.com: Skyscanner doesn't sell tickets — it's a search engine. When you search, Skyscanner scans 1,200+ airlines and travel sites — showing you all available prices — and redirects you to the cheapest site to complete the booking. This model has two advantages: you get a comprehensive view of all market prices instantly. And Skyscanner is neutral — it doesn't favor one airline over another because it doesn't own inventory. Any booking done through Skyscanner is actually completed on another site — meaning you don't create an account on Skyscanner unless you want extra features.

Smart Search Features — "Whole Month" and "Everywhere"

1. Whole Month Search

The most powerful feature in Skyscanner — not available on most competing sites. Instead of searching day by day, tell Skyscanner "show me prices for the whole month" and it gives you a full calendar of cheapest days. Example: search for Cairo to Tokyo in July. Skyscanner shows you it's 40% cheaper in the first week. You can choose a specific day as low as $800 instead of $1,200+.

2. Search "Everywhere"

Don't know where you want to go? Type "Everywhere" in the destination field — Skyscanner shows you all possible destinations sorted by price. Use case: you have $350 budget from Dubai — Skyscanner shows you can reach 12 destinations in Europe, 6 in Asia, and 8 in Africa — all within your budget. You discover destinations you hadn't thought of at affordable prices.

3. Price Alerts

Classic but powerful — Skyscanner sends you price alerts when the ticket price drops to a specific level. You set the price threshold (e.g., $400 for London — Dubai). You save 20-30% on average when you buy at the right time.

4. Multi-City

  • Best for: Travelers visiting multiple cities in one trip.
  • Examples: Cairo → Istanbul → London → Cairo.
  • Cheapest option: Often booking a multi-city ticket is cheaper than booking each leg separately.
  • Flexibility: Skyscanner lets you add up to 6 destinations (8 hours max).
  • Smart pricing: Shows you different combinations — sometimes starting from a different city saves 30%.

Flight Pricing — How Do Airlines Set Prices?

Skyscanner doesn't set prices itself. But knowing how airline pricing works helps you:

  • Booking timing: International flights — best 3-7 months before departure. Domestic flights — 2-4 weeks.
  • Peak seasons: Prices skyrocket during holidays (Christmas, Eid, summer).
  • Day of week: Tuesday and Wednesday are usually cheapest. Friday and Sunday are most expensive.
  • Budget airlines: Often significantly cheaper (Ryanair, Wizz Air, Air Arabia).

Integration with Booking.com and Expedia

Skyscanner is a search engine — it doesn't hold inventory. But after acquisition, it integrates more with Trip.com. However, the beauty of Skyscanner remains its neutrality: Skyscanner shows prices from Booking.com and Expedia in search results — just like any other booking site. The difference: Skyscanner displays the Booking.com or Expedia price directly. You decide which site to complete the booking on.

8 Tips to Save 40% on Flight Tickets

  1. Start with "Whole Month" search: Before choosing a specific date, open Skyscanner's monthly view. Just 3 days difference can save 30%.
  2. Use nearby airports: Skyscanner automatically shows nearby airports in results. A flight from a different airport (e.g., Milan Bergamo instead of Milan Malpensa) can save 200-400 USD.
  3. Enable price alerts: Set a price target and wait for an alert. Skyscanner monitors prices continuously.
  4. Use private browsing: Airline sites remember your search and may raise prices. Use Incognito mode.
  5. Consider multi-city wisely: Business travel (Mon-Wed-Thu) is more expensive than leisure (Tue-Thu-Sat). Try Multi-City on Skyscanner.
  6. Don't ignore budget airlines: Budget airlines offer excellent value for short and medium flights — sometimes 40% cheaper.
  7. Search for budget airlines directly: Skyscanner includes all budget airlines (Ryanair, Wizz Air, Air Arabia). Don't exclude them from results — they're often the cheapest.
  8. Compare with Kayak: Search both Skyscanner and Kayak for the same route — sometimes one finds a deal the other misses.
