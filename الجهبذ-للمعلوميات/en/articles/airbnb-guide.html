The Story of Airbnb: From an Air Mattress to a Sharing Economy Empire

Few startups have a founding story as simple — yet as brilliant — as Airbnb's. Imagine: in October 2007, Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia, two unemployed engineers in San Francisco, needed to pay rent. They found out a design conference was coming to town, and all hotels were fully booked. So they offered 3 guests — at $80 per person — to sleep on air mattresses in their living room. And they provided breakfast in the morning. Hence the name: Air Bed & Breakfast. Later shortened to Airbnb.

The beginning was catastrophic. The site launched in August 2008 — and nobody cared. The reason: poor design, weak marketing, and the idea of "sleeping at a stranger's" was strange to people. Brian and Joe went into debt. To fund the company, they sold presidential-themed breakfast cereals — "Obama O's" and "Cap'n McCain's" — during the 2008 election campaign. They raised $30,000. Not much, but it kept them alive.

The real breakthrough came in 2009 when Nathan Blecharczyk (current CTO) joined as a third partner — and they entered Y Combinator — the world's most famous startup accelerator. There, they developed the site, improved user experience, and added a professional photography system for hosts — a turning point: listings with professional photos receive 2-3 times more bookings.

Today, Airbnb is the largest accommodation company in the world — without owning a single hotel. Its market value is around $80 billion. It has 7.7 million active listings in 220 countries. And strangely, the idea of "sleeping at a stranger's" — which was weird in 2008 — became a global phenomenon.

Turning Point: In December 2020, Airbnb went public (IPO) at $68 per share. On the first trading day, the price jumped to $144 — the company was valued at $100 billion in an instant. For comparison: Marriott International — the largest hotel chain in the world — is valued at around $50 billion. Airbnb, which doesn't own a single hotel, was worth twice as much as Marriott with its 8,000 hotels.

How Airbnb Works — And Why Everything Changed in 2026

The Airbnb idea is simple: You (the host) have a spare room, apartment, or villa — list it for travelers. You (the guest) want a place to spend a night, week, or month — book it through the platform. Airbnb is the middleman — providing trust, payment system, insurance, and customer service.

But in 2026, things are very different from 2016. Laws in most major cities — New York, Paris, London, Barcelona, Dubai — have become much stricter on short-term rentals. In New York, for example, Local Law 18 effectively banned rentals under 30 days except in very specific circumstances. Barcelona began banning new tourist rental licenses. Paris imposed very high fees and limited nights to 120 per year per host.

Airbnb, on the other hand, responded quietly and focused on long-term stays (28 days+) — which now account for 24% of the company's revenue in 2025. It also launched Airbnb Rooms — a return to its roots (a room in someone's apartment, not a whole apartment) — at lower prices.

The Guest Experience — Pros and Risks

Pros

  • Space and privacy: A full apartment with kitchen and washing machine is much better than a hotel room — especially for families or long trips.
  • Local experience: Staying in a real neighborhood, not a tourist area — local communities, real restaurants, not an isolated hotel zone.
  • Flexibility: The kitchen saves food money (if traveling for two weeks, food savings can be about 40% of budget).
  • Competitive prices for groups: A villa for $200 for 8 people — cheaper than 4 hotel rooms at $100 each.

Risks

  • Quality not guaranteed: Hotels have uniform standards. Airbnb — host by host. Some are professional, some are not.
  • Cancellation: Cancellation policies vary by host — some are very strict.
  • Hidden fees: The price you see first is not the final price — there are cleaning fees and huge service fees (sometimes 30-40% extra).
  • Hidden cameras: Rare but exist — Airbnb banned cameras inside rooms from 2024 but the problem still exists in some places.
  • Fraud: Fake listings — hosts asking for transfers outside the platform — beware.
Important Warning: Never transfer money outside the Airbnb platform. Any host asking you to transfer a deposit or additional payment via WhatsApp or bank transfer — report them immediately and inform the platform. Airbnb only provides fraud protection if the booking and payment are made within the platform.

The Host Experience — Is It Still Worthwhile?

Airbnb made millions around the world turn their properties into an extra income source. In 2019, some hosts in Dubai, for example, were earning 30,000 AED monthly from a single villa. But the situation in 2026 is different:

The numbers show a decline in profitability. The main reason: saturation — many more people became hosts, supply increased, prices dropped. With new regulations in major cities, investing in Airbnb is no longer as guaranteed as before. But it's still viable in ongoing tourist destinations and in cities where regulations are still flexible (such as some Asian and Latin American cities).

Pricing — Is Airbnb Really Cheaper Than a Hotel?

The answer: It depends. If you're traveling alone or with a partner — a hotel is often cheaper (or the same price) without cleaning and service fees. If you're traveling with a family of 4-6 people — an Airbnb apartment is significantly cheaper.

Practical example: A family of 5 traveling to Paris for 7 nights, July 2026:

OptionTotal CostCost per person per night
Airbnb apartment (3 bedrooms)€2,100 (€1,500 rent + €350 fees + €250 cleaning)€60
Hotel — 2 rooms (Booking.com)€3,500€100
Hotel — 1 large room€2,100€60 (but cramped)
Entire villa (Booking.com)€2,800€80

The bottom line: Airbnb wins for families and groups. For couples and individuals — Booking.com and Expedia offer better deals.

The Affiliate Program — And Why It No Longer Exists

Very important information for any publisher or travel blogger: Airbnb officially closed its affiliate program in 2022. The decision surprised many — Airbnb had one of the most famous affiliate programs in the travel industry with commissions up to 30% on new bookings. But the company decided to shut it down.

The official reason: redirecting resources toward other marketing channels. The unofficial reason (analytically): Airbnb reached a level of brand maturity where it no longer needs affiliate channels — people see a TV ad and search for Airbnb directly. Also, Airbnb focused on building direct strategic partnerships with companies (like Delta Airlines and Qantas) instead of open affiliate programs.

What does this mean for the average user? You won't find discount links or promo codes from influencers and bloggers — because there's no affiliate system supporting them. Anyone claiming to have an "Airbnb discount code" is likely outdated or inactive. The only way to get a discount on Airbnb is through direct offers from the platform itself (like first-booking discounts in some countries) or through cashback programs from platforms like TopCashback and Honey.

Verdict: When to Choose Airbnb and When to Stay Away

Choose Airbnb if: Traveling with family/large group, need a kitchen and space, want a real local experience, traveling for two weeks or more
Avoid Airbnb if: Traveling alone or as a couple, need hotel services (24hr reception, room service), can't handle unexpected quality surprises, traveling for business
When we prefer Booking.com: When priority is trust, uniform quality, flexible cancellation, and a loyalty program — with the option of apartments and villas too (Booking.com has 28 million properties)
Final Verdict: Airbnb is not a "hotel alternative" — it's a completely different category. Using it correctly depends on your trip type. Understanding the difference between the two products is the key to a successful travel experience.
Al-Jehabiz Team Recommendation: For long family trips — Airbnb is the best choice. For business or short trips — use Booking.com or Expedia. And if you choose Airbnb, read all the negative reviews first — not the positive ones — to know exactly what problems to expect.
