The Fog Creek Story — From Bug Tracker to Kanban Board

In 2011, Joel Spolsky (founder of Fog Creek Software and Stack Overflow) had a problem: his development team needed a simple way to track tasks. All existing tools (Jira, Bugzilla) were complex. He said: "Why do we build tools that complicate people's lives? Let's build something that looks like the whiteboard in our office."

Thus Trello was born. The idea was inspired by the Kanban board — 3 columns: To Do, Doing, Done. But Trello added: colors, background images, comments, due dates, and instant sharing. In 2017, Atlassian (the same company that owns Jira and Bitbucket) bought it for $425 million.

Trello took inspiration from the Kanban system — a Toyota production system. The idea: tasks move from one column to another. Trello brought this philosophy to the average user — and created 50 million users.

What Exactly Is Trello?

Trello is a visual task management tool — you can organize anything in Boards, each Board has Lists, and each List has Cards. This simplicity is its secret to success: in 5 minutes you can understand everything.

Use cases: project management, personal planning, hiring, content, sales.

Boards, Lists, and Cards

Components of a single card: description with Markdown, checklist, comments, due date, color labels, members, attachments, Power-Ups.

Lists can be stages: "Not Started, In Progress, Review, Done" — or sections: "Marketing, Design, Development."

Butler — No-Code Automation

In 2018, Atlassian acquired Butler and added it as part of the plans. It works with If This Then That: when a card moves to a "Done" column — send a Slack notification. Every Monday — create a new card. The free plan allows 25 automations. Premium is unlimited.

Power-Ups — Super Integrations

200+ add-ons for advanced features: Calendar, Card Repeater, Custom Fields, Slack, Google Drive, Jira, Time Tracking, Voting.

On the free plan: 1 Power-Up per board. On paid: unlimited.
