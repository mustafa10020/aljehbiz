The Beginning — The Tale of the Godfather Who Grew Old

In the world of SEO tools, one tool existed before anyone else. Moz (originally called SEOmoz) started in 2004 — before Google Panda and Penguin, before SEO was even "a thing." Before Ahrefs (2010) and before Semrush (2008 as an idea). Moz is the godfather of modern SEO tools.

But in 2026, the question everyone asks: Is Moz still relevant?

When you compare Moz with Ahrefs which has 43 trillion backlinks, with Moz which has only 4.5 trillion — the difference is shocking. When you see Semrush with 55 comprehensive tools covering SEO, PPC, Social, and Content — Moz has only 8 tools. So does anyone still use it? And why do people pay $49/month for Moz instead of $99 for Ahrefs?

The answer isn't simple. Moz in 2026 isn't trying to compete on data quantity — because it lost that battle since 2018. Moz now focuses on ease of use, accuracy in the metrics it has, and community support. The question: Are those 3 things enough to sustain an SEO tool in 2026?

Moz started as an SEO blog in 2004 — not as a tool. Its founder Rand Fishkin (the most famous person in SEO for years) wrote content that built the company's reputation. MozBar was the first browser extension for SEO analysis. The first Domain Authority tool. The first backlink analysis tool available to beginners. Moz isn't just a tool — it's part of SEO history itself.

Moz's Story — From a Blog in a Garage to an Empire

Moz's story began in 2004. Rand Fishkin and Gillian Muessig started an SEO consulting company in Seattle — but Rand had an unusual curiosity. He wrote a daily blog about SEO — deep educational content — which attracted followers. In 2007, they launched SEOmoz — the first link analysis tool on the market.

2012-2014 were Moz's glory days. They were the #1 SEO tool. Domain Authority (DA) became the most famous metric in the entire market. MozBar extension was installed on a million browsers. The Moz Blog was the #1 reference for learning SEO in the world.

But in 2015, problems began. Ahrefs had a larger database. Semrush was adding features quickly. Moz — slow to update. The database wasn't growing at the same speed. In 2018 they lost significant market share.

In 2021, Ziff Davis (owner of PCMag, Mashable) bought the company — which changed the company's dynamics. They became part of a media group. The advantage: financial stability. The disadvantage: innovation speed remains slow.

The bottom line: Moz isn't like Ahrefs — a specialized company led by engineers. Moz is an old company, has gone through many transformations, and doesn't evolve at the same speed. But that doesn't mean its product isn't useful — it might be useful for a certain category of users.

Domain Authority — What Made Moz Famous (And Controversial)

Domain Authority (DA) is the most famous metric Moz launched — and the main reason people still use Moz today.

DA is a score from 1 to 100 — measuring site strength based on the quality and quantity of backlinks. Strong sites: DA 80+ (CNN 93, Wikipedia 99). Medium sites: DA 40-60. New sites: DA 1-20.

Why is DA famous? Because it's easy to understand. Instead of analyzing 50 backlinks and complex metrics, DA gives you one number indicating relative strength.

On the flip side, why is DA controversial?

  • DA isn't a Google metric. Google doesn't use it for ranking. It's just a Moz indicator — their own.
  • Easy to manipulate. PBNs (Private Blog Networks) can artificially raise DA.
  • Slow to update. DA updates every 30 days — while backlinks change every hour.
  • Not accurate for small sites. A small site's DA might be 5 — giving the impression the site is very weak even if its content quality is high.

Comparison: Ahrefs has DR (Domain Rating) — same idea but with a different formula and updates faster. DR has the same issues as DA — not a Google metric. The only difference: Ahrefs' database is larger, so DR is often more accurate.

Use DA with caution: as a relative indicator, not absolute. "DA 40 vs DA 50" — the difference isn't necessarily big. But "DA 20 vs DA 80" — that's a real difference.

Keyword Explorer — A Pleasant Surprise

Moz's Keyword Explorer tool — despite its smaller data set (only 1 billion keywords compared to 12 billion in Ahrefs) — excels in other areas:

  • Priority Score: A composite metric combining Volume, Difficulty, CTR to determine "the best keyword to target." You can filter by "Organic CTR" to see keywords that actually get clicks.
  • Difficulty Score Accuracy: Keyword Difficulty in Moz is on a 1-100 scale — and it's accurate because Moz considers content quality in the top 10 results, not just backlink strength. For low-medium difficulty keywords (1-40), Moz is more accurate than Ahrefs.
  • SERP Analysis: Analyzes the top 50 SERP results — not just the top 10 — and tells you what features appeared.
  • List Building: Create unlimited lists and organize them.

The biggest problem: limited keywords. If you have a narrow keyword niche or low volume keywords — Moz might not have data on them. For specialized "long tail" keywords, Ahrefs or Semrush have more coverage.

Link Explorer — Decent, But Not Ahrefs

Link Explorer is Moz's backlink analysis tool. Its database has 4.5 trillion links — a big number but not close to Ahrefs' 43 trillion.

Things Moz does well:

  • Spam Score: A metric measuring the likelihood a site is spam — from 1 to 100. PBN sites typically have high Spam Scores. The feature isn't 100% accurate but useful for filtering.
  • Link Intersect: Enter your domain and 3 competitors — and see sites linking to all of them except you. An opportunity for direct Outreach.
  • Top Pages: The site's most popular pages by backlinks — not by traffic — a difference from Ahrefs which shows by traffic.
  • Linking Domains Distribution: Quality distribution of domains linking to you — how many DA 80+ and how many DA 20-

The big difference with Ahrefs: update speed. Ahrefs updates backlinks every 15 minutes. Moz every 24 hours. If you work in professional Link Building — a backlink can appear and disappear in a day. Moz won't catch up. But for weekly monitoring — Moz gets the job done.

MozBar — Every SEO's Secret Weapon

MozBar is a free Chrome browser extension from Moz — and it's the secret weapon that Moz isn't as famous for as it deserves.

Once you install the extension (free), every page you open in Chrome shows:

  • DA and PA (Page Authority) of the page
  • Number of backlinks
  • Number of Linking Domains
  • Spam Score
  • Keywords the page ranks for (on click)

What's PA? Page Authority — same idea as DA but at the page level, not the domain. Some pages on a weak DA site can have high PA if they have strong backlinks pointing to them.

Common usage: when browsing a competitor's result and wondering "How are they ranking before this?" — MozBar gives you their site's DA and the exact number of backlinks for that page.

No other tool has this level of simplicity in a free browser extension. Ahrefs has the Ahrefs Toolbar — but it's not as widespread. Semrush doesn't have a free browser extension like MozBar. MozBar is Moz's biggest asset in 2026 — and the thing people miss most about Moz when they don't have it.

Site Crawl — Simple Technical Audit

Site Crawl in Moz is the simplest technical audit tool in the market — and that's an advantage if you're a beginner.

Its basic features:

  • Scan 30,000 pages (in the highest package)
  • Report on errors and technical issues — 30+ issues (compared to 170+ in Ahrefs and Semrush)
  • Fix recommendations — simple and direct
  • Periodic email reports

The problem: not as deep as Ahrefs or Semrush in Technical SEO. If your site is large (100,000+ pages) — Moz's Site Crawl won't be enough. You need a stronger tool. But if your site is small (under 10,000 pages) — Moz's fewer errors are easier to understand than 170 errors in Ahrefs.

Local SEO & Listings — The Unexpected Star

In Local SEO, Moz has a tool called Moz Local — and this is an area neither Ahrefs nor Semrush covers with the same strength.

Moz Local helps you:

  • Unify your business information (NAP: Name, Address, Phone) across 40+ business directories (Google Business Profile, Facebook, Yelp, Bing Places...)
  • Monitor reviews across all platforms
  • Track your Local Pack ranking (Google Maps)
  • Fix errors in your business data

In 2026, 46% of Google searches have local intent (Search Engine Land). Local SEO isn't a luxury — it's a necessity for stores, restaurants, clinics, and services. Moz Local is the strongest Local SEO tool compared to anything in Ahrefs (which has no Local Tools at all) and Semrush (which has Listing Management but not as deep as Moz Local).
