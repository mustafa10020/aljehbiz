The Story of Booking.com: From a Dutch Garage to a Booking Empire

Imagine a simple idea — "Why isn't there a site that lets me book a hotel from any country in the world with one click?" — launched from a car garage in Amsterdam, and became an empire worth over $130 billion. This isn't fiction, this is the story of Booking.com.

In 1996, Dutch founder Geert-Jan Bruinsma launched a small company called Booking B.V. with a modest office in Amsterdam. The idea was clear: connect small European hotels with travelers from around the world. Back then, the internet was still in its infancy — most hotels didn't even have websites. Booking.com served as their first "digital home."

The pivotal year was 2005. Priceline Group (the American company that also owns Priceline.com and Kayak) acquired Booking.com for $133 million. At the time, the deal raised eyebrows — "$133 million for a small European booking site?!" — but the result: Booking.com transformed from a struggling startup into a money-printing machine. The return on investment? More than 1,000 times.

Under the Priceline umbrella (later renamed Booking Holdings in 2018), the site expanded massively. The group acquired Agoda (2007), and while Expedia is different — Booking Holdings bought Agoda, Kayak, and OpenTable. The point is, Booking.com became the crown jewel. Today, Booking Holdings is the largest travel booking company in the world with a market value exceeding $130 billion.

Turning Point: In 2018, Priceline Group changed its name to Booking Holdings — acknowledging that Booking.com was the strongest brand in the group. That same year, annual bookings on the platform exceeded $90 billion. Ninety billion — more than the GDP of many countries.

What Makes Booking.com Different?

In a world full of booking sites — Expedia, Agoda, Hotels.com, Trivago — why is Booking.com the most famous globally? The answer isn't just in massive marketing, but in the experience itself.

First: Massive Variety. Booking.com features over 28 million property listings — hotels, apartments, villas, resorts, countryside homes, and even tents and treehouses. No other platform comes close to this number. Expedia, for example, has about 3 million properties. The difference is stark.

Second: User Interface. Objectively, Booking.com spent enormous amounts on user experience. The search is intuitive, filters are precise, images are high quality, and reviews are organized. You can pinpoint — "a 4-star hotel in central Paris, with a pool and free WiFi, rated above 8.5, under $200 per night" — in 3 clicks.

Third: Book Without a Card — In Some Places. The "book now, pay later" feature with free cancellation is available at millions of properties. Not all hotels offer it, but Booking.com promotes it heavily.

Genius Loyalty: The Smartest Rewards Program

The Genius program is Booking.com's loyalty system. Subscribed? No, it's completely free. The more you book, the higher you level up.

The truth: Genius isn't perfect. The discount isn't on all hotels — many hotels don't offer a Genius discount at all. And some hotels offer 10% off but the base price is inflated so the discount brings it back to the normal rate. You need to compare.

Tip: Create a Genius account (free, of course) before you search. 10% off, even on some properties, is better than nothing. Try to book 5 times in 2 years — if you're a frequent traveler, Level 2 is worth the effort.

Cancellation Policy & Flexibility — A Double-Edged Sword

One of Booking.com's strongest features — and at the same time one of its biggest traps — is the cancellation policy. The site is known for flexibility: most bookings can be canceled free of charge within a specific period (usually 24-48 hours before arrival). But the pitfall: book now, pay later makes you book impulsively and forget to cancel. You end up paying a penalty or the cost of the first night.

Booking.com always shows two options: "Free cancellation" and "Non-refundable." The first is usually 10-20% more expensive. The second is cheaper, but if your plans change, you lose your money. The rule: if your trip is 100% confirmed — choose non-refundable. If you're unsure — pay a little extra and sleep soundly.

Hidden Features Most Users Don't Know

1. Price Match

Many people don't know that Booking.com has a limited price match policy. If you find the same hotel — same room, same dates — on another site for less, Booking.com may match the price and give you the difference. The service isn't automatic — you need to call customer support. Success isn't guaranteed, but it's worth trying, especially for large bookings.

2. Filter by "Sustainable Rating"

Since 2023, Booking.com added sustainability filters — hotels with environmental certifications, reduced plastic use, renewable energy. If you're into responsible tourism, this is great.

3. Business Travel (Booking.com for Business)

A separate platform for employees and companies. You can manage your team's bookings, control the budget, and issue unified tax invoices. Free for small businesses.

4. Voice Booking & Digital Assistant

Yes — you can say "Hey Booking, find me a hotel in Dubai next weekend" and the app will handle it. The feature is still in early stages but promising.

10 Pro Tips to Save Money on Booking.com

  1. Log In Before You Search: Genius prices only show when you're logged in. A large percentage of people browse the site without logging in — and pay more.
  2. Use Incognito Mode: There's evidence that Booking.com uses cookies to raise prices slightly if you visit the same hotel multiple times. Open an incognito window and compare.
  3. Search in Local Currency: If traveling, change the currency to your destination's currency. Sometimes local currency prices are lower than converted dollar prices — especially in countries like Turkey and Egypt.
  4. Book Last Minute: Hotels want to fill empty rooms. A couple of days before travel, prices often drop sharply — the "Tonight" feature (Genius Deals) is excellent.
  5. Filter "Genius Deals" First: On the search page, select the deals filter — Genius deals often offer real, not fake, discounts.
  6. Don't Trust an 8.0 Rating Blindly: Booking.com's 1-10 rating system is a bit odd. Any hotel above 8.5 is excellent. 8.0-8.5 is very good. Below 8, there's an issue.
  7. Call the Hotel Directly After Booking: We've heard many cases — not an official rule — booking on Booking.com can help negotiate an upgrade or extra discount if you call the hotel right after booking.
  8. Read the Cancellation Policy in Detail: Not every "free cancellation" is truly free. In many cases, free cancellation deducts a small admin fee. Read the terms.
  9. Use the Mobile App: The Booking.com app often offers exclusive deals not available on the regular site — especially Genius Tonight deals.
  10. Compare Before Final Booking: Always — literally always — open Agoda, Expedia, and the hotel's official site before clicking "Confirm Booking." 5 minutes of comparison can save you 20-30%.
