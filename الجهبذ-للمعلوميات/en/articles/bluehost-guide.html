In the world of hosting, few names enjoy the trust of beginners like Bluehost. Founded in 2003, officially recommended by WordPress.org for over a decade, serving millions of websites worldwide. But the question that asserts itself in 2026: With stronger, faster, and cheaper competitors emerging — is Bluehost still worth being the top choice?

In this in-depth review, we analyze Bluehost from every angle: the story, real performance (with our test numbers), hidden prices, cPanel, support quality, and why professionals move away from it while beginners flock to it. We also directly compare it with its strongest competitors: Hostinger and SiteGround.

Evaluation Methodology: We purchased a Choice Plus plan from Bluehost (prepaid for 12 months), and hosted a test WordPress site. We monitored it for 30 days via UptimeRobot with checks every 5 minutes, and daily GTmetrix and Pingdom tests. We tested technical support 3 times via chat and once via phone.

Bluehost's Story: How It Became the Most Trusted Name in Hosting

Bluehost was founded in 2003 in Orem, Utah, USA. Founder Matt Heaton was a web developer who noticed a gap in the market: reliable and easy hosting for WordPress users — which was just beginning to spread.

In 2010, the biggest shift happened: Endurance International Group (EIG) — now Newfold Digital — acquired Bluehost. EIG also owns HostGator, iPage, FatCow, and others. This acquisition was controversial: some considered it the end of Bluehost's "golden age," as support quality began to decline with cost-cutting efforts.

But in 2016, something unexpected happened: WordPress.org removed HostGator's recommendation from its site and kept only Bluehost. This was a massive vote of confidence and made Bluehost almost monopolize the beginner WordPress market.

Fact: Bluehost is one of the few companies that hosts WordPress.org's own servers. Yes — WordPress.org and WordPress.com servers partially run on Bluehost's infrastructure. This explains why WordPress.org trusts them: they are genuine technical partners.

WordPress.org Endorsement: A Double-Edged Sword

The WordPress.org recommendation is Bluehost's strongest marketing weapon. When a beginner goes to WordPress.org and clicks "Get Hosting," the first name they see is Bluehost. This creates an instant impression of trust: "WordPress itself trusts them, so they can't be bad."

But the recommendation has a dark side. Many users — especially in r/webhosting communities on Reddit — complain that the recommendation is indirectly paid for. WordPress.org doesn't charge money directly, but Bluehost pays for being the official sponsor of the WordPress project and provides free infrastructure. Practically, the recommendation has become closer to a "service exchange deal" than a neutral recommendation.

Most importantly: The recommendation does not mean Bluehost is the fastest, cheapest, or best. It only means it's the easiest for beginners. And this difference is subtle but fundamental.

Performance and Speed: The Biggest Weakness

This is Bluehost's Achilles' heel. While Hostinger offers LiteSpeed servers and SiteGround uses Google Cloud, Bluehost still relies on traditional Apache servers — technology over 25 years old.

cPanel: Why It's Still Magical Despite Its Age

cPanel is the "classic interface" for hosting management. It's over 25 years old and still the most widely used worldwide. Bluehost offers cPanel in its traditional form — which is good for users coming from other hosting companies, but bad in terms of modern experience.

cPanel in Bluehost: The Good and Bad

  • Easy if you know it: If you've used cPanel before, you'll feel right at home
  • Comprehensive tools: Everything you need in one place — DNS, databases, email, files
  • Softaculous: One-click app installer supporting over 400 applications

But compared to Hostinger's hPanel or SiteGround's Site Tools, cPanel looks old. The interface is cluttered, navigation between menus is slow, and the main control panel reminds me of Windows 95 ads.

Technical Support: Phone Support to the Rescue

In terms of support, Bluehost offers 24/7 service via live chat and phone. This is an advantage not available with Hostinger (chat only) or SiteGround (chat only).

We tested support:

  • Live chat: Response after 3 minutes. The reply was helpful but felt like it came from a "ready script" rather than a real tech expert. Took 8 minutes to solve a simple issue (changing PHP version).
  • Phone support: We called the toll-free number. Waited 2 minutes. The agent was friendly and helpful — but English only. Problem solved in 5 minutes.

Support is acceptable, but not exceptional. SiteGround's support is higher quality — but in English. Hostinger offers faster Arabic support but without phone.

Verdict: Is It Still Worth It?

Al-Jehabiz Verdict

Buy Bluehost if: You are a complete beginner in hosting and WordPress, want a solution that "works out of the box" without complications, and need phone support (even in English). The trust that WordPress.org recommendation gives is invaluable for new users.
Look at Hostinger if: You want faster performance at half the price, need Arabic support, and can do without phone. Hostinger beats Bluehost in almost everything except "reputation."
Look at SiteGround if: You run a business project needing guaranteed stability (99.99%) and high performance on Google Cloud, and your budget allows $10+/month.
Al-Jehabiz Summary: Bluehost isn't bad — but it's no longer the best choice. In 2026, it's hard to recommend hosting at $7.45/month that delivers 3.4-second performance when Hostinger gives you 2.6-second performance at $2.99. Bluehost's only remaining strength is reputation and the WordPress.org recommendation. If you're a beginner wanting "safety," Bluehost is a safe choice. But if you want real value for money, Hostinger or SiteGround are better options.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Bluehost better than Hostinger?

For the Arab audience — no. Hostinger is faster (LiteSpeed vs Apache), cheaper ($2.99 vs $4.95), and offers Arabic support. Bluehost only excels in reputation (WordPress.org recommendation) and phone support. Check the Complete Hostinger Guide for detailed comparison.

Is the WordPress.org recommendation for Bluehost still valid?

Yes, the recommendation is still on WordPress.org. But understanding that it means "easiest for beginners" and not "best performance" is an important distinction. For advanced users, Hostinger or SiteGround are better choices.

Does Bluehost support Arabic websites?

Technically yes — but there's no Arabic support, and no data center in the Middle East (its only data center is in the US). This means higher latency for Arab users. If your audience is Arabic, Hostinger or SiteGround are geographically better choices.

What's the best Bluehost plan for beginners?

We recommend Choice Plus. The promotional price of $7.45/month gives you unlimited sites, 40GB SSD storage, automatic backups, and free domain privacy. Basic is limited to one site only.

Can I move my site from Bluehost to Hostinger?

Yes, easily. Hostinger provides free migration from Bluehost. Their team will move your site within 24 hours with no downtime. This service is free for all Hostinger plans.
