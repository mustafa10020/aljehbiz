The Story of Tripadvisor: From a Small Paper Guide to a Review Giant

Some travel sites started as an idea in a garage. Tripadvisor started as an idea in a kitchen. The kitchen of Stephen Kaufer in Newton, Massachusetts, in the year 2000. Stephen and his wife were planning a trip to Italy — and found it extremely difficult to get reliable information about hotels and restaurants. Paper catalogs from travel agencies were outdated. Lonely Planet guides were limited. Forums were scattered. Stephen — a software engineer — said: "Let's build a site that puts all this in one place."

Tripadvisor officially launched in 2001 — the same year the dot-com bubble burst. A catastrophic timing on paper. But the idea was stronger than the timing. The site relied on user-generated content (UGC) — reviews of hotels, restaurants, and attractions from real travelers. In those days, this was revolutionary. People were used to trusting professional writers — suddenly any ordinary traveler could write their opinion and have millions see it.

The real boom was in 2005-2008 when global tourism exploded. People started traveling more, and started relying on Tripadvisor reviews more than any other source. In 2008, the site surpassed 10 million reviews. In 2014 — 100 million. Today (2026): more than 1 billion reviews and opinions, covering 8 million properties, restaurants, and attractions in 190 countries.

The interesting story: Tripadvisor started as a review-only site — it didn't book anything. It made money from ads and affiliate links. But over time, it added direct booking services — and today you can book your hotels, tours, and even restaurant tickets through Tripadvisor. It transformed from just a "guide" to an "integrated travel platform."

Turning Point: In 2019, Airbnb — yes Airbnb — almost bought Tripadvisor. Negotiations reached advanced stages, but the deal fell through at the last moment due to price disagreements. If the deal had gone through, the travel industry would look completely different today. Two years later, in 2021, Booking Holdings announced it had acquired Tripadvisor for $3.5 billion.

The Review System: Can You Trust a Five-Star Rating?

Tripadvisor's rating system seems simple on the surface: from 1 star (terrible) to 5 stars (excellent). The total is the average of all reviews. But behind this simplicity lies a complex world of algorithms, fake reviews, and misleading content.

The Problem of Fake Reviews

Yes — they exist in abundance. Hotels pay people to write positive reviews. Competing hotels pay people to write negative reviews. Tripadvisor has a full team of analysts and an AI system to detect fake reviews. According to their reports, in 2025, they blocked more than 30 million suspicious reviews before publication. But blocking a review isn't the same as preventing it — published reviews remain under monitoring.

How to Spot a Real Review?

Don't just read reviews — study them. You need to look at the pattern: a hotel rated 4.5 with all comments from new accounts writing poor English? Not trustworthy. A hotel rated 4.0 with hundreds of reviews over 3 years, including both positive and negative? That's the real one. Read the negative reviews first — see if the complaint is recurring (cleanliness? bed? noise?) — or just a picky customer.

Also: Tripadvisor places a "Travellers' Choice" badge on top-rated hotels. This is an annual classification based on the quantity and quality of reviews, not just the average. If a hotel has a "Travellers' Choice Best of the Best" badge — it's among the top 1% in the world.

Booking Holdings Acquisition — What Does It Mean?

At the end of 2021, Booking Holdings — the parent company of Booking.com, Agoda, Kayak, and Priceline — acquired Tripadvisor for $3.5 billion. The deal sparked widespread debate: would Tripadvisor lose its neutrality as a review platform after falling under the umbrella of the market's biggest competitor?

From a practical standpoint, Booking Holdings said Tripadvisor would continue operating as an independent entity with its original review algorithm intact. But realistically — the integration between the two platforms is clearly increasing. Bookings are processed through Booking.com technology in the background. The offers shown on Tripadvisor are sometimes powered by Expedia's database. Integration is horizontal — the group benefits from all its assets.

The biggest impact was in data. Booking Holdings now has the largest repository of traveler behavior data in the world — from search (Kayak and Tripadvisor) to booking (Booking.com and Agoda) to review (Tripadvisor). This triple data gives them a competitive advantage no other company can match.

Tripadvisor Forum: The Traveler's Hidden Treasure

Most Tripadvisor users focus on reviews and photos — and miss the forum gem. Tripadvisor Forums are the largest user-generated travel repository in the world. In every city, there's a dedicated thread answered by "Local Experts" — people living in that city who answer travelers' questions.

Examples: "Best restaurant in Marrakech for $50 per person?" — answered by locals. "Is the Khaosan Road area in Bangkok safe for families?" — answered by forum experts. "Best time to visit Sharm El-Sheikh to avoid crowds?" — instant responses from the travel community.

The rules: Ask your question clearly, politely, and wait for responses from local experts. Not all are experts — some respond without experience — but the collection of responses gives you a comprehensive picture.

Smart Travel Tools from Tripadvisor

1. Travellers' Choice Awards

Annually, Tripadvisor releases "Travellers' Choice" lists for the best hotels, islands, beaches, and restaurants in the world. In 2026, the rankings included: Top 25 hotels in the world, Top 10 islands (Bali #1 as always), Top 10 fine dining restaurants. Very useful if you're planning a trip from scratch.

2. Vacation Rentals

After the acquisition, Tripadvisor added an entire section for short-term rentals — a direct competitor to Airbnb. You can book an apartment or entire villa, with Tripadvisor's traditional reviews.

3. Things to Do

Book tours and activities directly — from a safari in Dubai to Louvre Museum tickets in Paris. This section stands out by showing prices from multiple operators — you compare and choose the best.

4. Forums + AI Integration

In 2024, Tripadvisor added an AI assistant for forums — summarizing responses and suggesting the most helpful answers. Not perfect, but helps guide you to the right response quickly.

Tripadvisor Pros

  • Largest review platform in the world — 1B+ reviews and opinions
  • Most comprehensive restaurant and attraction information
  • Active forum with local experts in almost every destination
  • Travellers' Choice Awards — reliable annual ranking
  • Book tours and activities from multiple operators
  • Excellent Arabic support and easy interface

Tripadvisor Cons

  • Fake reviews are still a problem — needs scrutiny
  • Forum experts are not always neutral (some are hosts)
  • Booking service is less mature than Booking.com
  • No loyalty program to reward regular users
  • After Booking Holdings acquisition — review neutrality questioned
  • Some features are paid (hotel ads appear before reviews)

Verdict: Is Tripadvisor Still the Top Reference?

Reviews: Tripadvisor is the world's top reference — no platform matches its volume and quality of review content
Best for research: Use it as a first step for any trip — search, read reviews, ask in the forum
Booking: Book through Booking.com after researching on Tripadvisor — best prices and better loyalty program
Weakness: Direct booking hasn't matured yet — and the review platform requires user awareness to distinguish real from fake
Al-Jehabiz Team Recommendation: Start your trip on Tripadvisor — search for your hotel, read 5 negative and 5 positive reviews, ask in the forum. Then open Booking.com and Expedia and compare prices. Never book on Tripadvisor without comparing — prices are often matched but Booking.com may have a Genius discount.
